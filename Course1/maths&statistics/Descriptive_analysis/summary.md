This is the most important warning!
Mean can trick you if there are extreme numbers.
Famous Candy Story Gone Wrong:
Four friends have: 5, 5, 5, 5 candies.
Mean = 5. Perfect! Everyone happy.
Now a super rich kid joins with 100 candies.
Numbers: 5, 5, 5, 5, 100
Add up: 120 √∑ 5 = 24 mean.
"Wow! On average, everyone has 24 candies!"
But really? Four kids still have only 5, one has 100. Most kids feel poor!
Median? Line up: 5,5,5,5,100 ‚Üí Middle is 5. Tells the true story!
Mode = 5.
See? Mean "lied" because of one big number.
This happens in real life:

Average salary in a company: Boss earns millions, workers earn little ‚Üí Average looks high, but most people earn low.
"Average house price" in a city with some mansions.

Always check all three: Mean, Median, Mode ‚Äî they tell different parts of the story!
Sometimes median is better for "typical" person.

## In-Depth Explanation of summary.md
This file warns about averages lying, using the candy story to show mean distortion. It includes classwork and exercises on mean, median, mode.

**Expanded Details**:
- **Core Warning**: Measures of central tendency can mislead if not contextualized. Mean assumes symmetry; outliers break this.
- **The Candy Example**: Illustrates Simpson's paradox in simple terms ‚Äî mean hides inequality.
- **Why Averages Lie**:
  - Skewness: Right-skewed data (e.g., incomes) pulls mean right; median stays central.
  - Outliers: One extreme value dominates (e.g., celebrity salaries inflate averages).
  - Sample Bias: Non-representative data leads to false "averages."
- **Balancing Measures**: Always compare mean, median, mode. If they differ, investigate (e.g., median < mean indicates right skew).
- **Classwork Breakdown**:
  - Test Scores: Close values show normal distribution; mean/median/mode align.
  - Extreme Example: Mean 164 vs. median 30 ‚Äî highlights robustness.
- **Exercises**: Reinforces concepts; encourages verbal/written practice.
- **Broader Lessons**: Teaches critical thinking ‚Äî stats aren't absolute truths. In data science, this prevents poor decisions (e.g., policy based on misleading averages).
- **Practical Tips**: Use visualizations (e.g., box plots) to spot lies. In Excel/Python, compute all three easily.
- **Advanced Note**: This ties into variance/standard deviation for spread. For deeper analysis, consider interquartile range (IQR) to quantify outlier impact.
